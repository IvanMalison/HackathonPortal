FAQ

1) When is it?

3/21/2013 - 3/22/2013

We'll start on Thursday and finish around 3:00pm Friday. At 3:30pm, we'll do presentations. Watch out for emails from engrec for more specific schedule info.

We'll provide plenty of meals, snacks and caffeine during the hack-a-thon.

There's also a mixer Monday 3/18 6-7pm on the 10th floor to help you find partners to hack with. Pizza will be provided.

2) Who's invited to hack at the hack-a-thon?

Everyone in product, systems and developers (interns included).

3) What can I work on?

Whatever you want! Scratch your developer itches. Write cool stuff. Build a wooden box if the mood strikes you. If you need some suggestions:

  • Visualization or other views - we have a lot great data that can be mined
  • Widgets
  • Cool examples of our public API
  • Tools to make your or your fellow developer lives easier
  • Mehfil feed -- err, perhaps this one is already done

4) If I build a cool product feature, will we launch it?

Product features still have to go through the product group to get released on public site. Don't let that discourage you from building your ideas -- you never know what might interest the team.

5) Can I start hacking before the hack-a-thon?

Sure can -- but do it on your own time.

6) Can I hack by myself?

We're a team here at Yelp, not a bunch of lone gunmen. Let's try for teams of 2-3. If you need help finding a partner, check out the mixer details in the answer to 1). You can also use this page to post your ideas and solicit help. If all else fails, just show up on Thursday morning and ask to join a team that seems cool.

7) Awards?

We have five awards categories. Winners will be voted on by you and your fellow engineers. The votes will be tallied and votes will be distributed sometime later.

  • Useful
  • Funny
  • Cool
  • Hardcore
  • Unhack

8) Hack-a-thon, Hackathon, HackAThon, Hack@thon. Which one is canonical?

Our jargon has the consistency of 18th century English.

9) Problems, Issues, or Ideas?

Come talk to Jason (jfennell), KB (ashleykb) or Pita (pcheng), since we are making this happen.

Hackathon Dataset UPDATED

We have a hackathon dataset available for you to make your data-related projects with.

This has recent data from prod for a lot of useful tables including reviews, checkins, quicktips, users, businesses, friendships and menus!!

Its all stored in S3. You can download a few chunks of it onto your local machine to develop with, and then point your map reduce job to the full dataset while running it on the EMR to quickly and easily process all of it.

You can read about it ​here.

Contact ​aditya@yelp.com with questions.

  • (​luther@yelp.com) Don't Be Evil -- Using Google Organic to Build a Fairer Google / Extending ​http://FocusOnTheUser.org's Open Source Browser Extension to Encompass Local
    • Problem: Today, the regulators providing the essential scrutiny to protect Mountain View from bulldozing the 3rd party content ecosystem (i.e. Yelp) simply don't get it. They're not technologists, and they get lost in the often cerebral conversations about why Google's behavior in Local is anti-competitive and anti-consumer. They are confused. I (Luther) saw it first-hand in Washington, DC (where Google got FTC to fold) and see it today in Brussels and with state attorneys general staff.
    • Solution: Give regulators (and consumers / media) a "simulator" or plug-in which imagines a pro-competitive Google. They need something they can "touch" and experience. Explaining it simply isn't working -- they need the capability of imagining a pro-competitive world and see for themselves why filling OneBoxes? from organic is better for consumers and the marketplace. This hackathon project will involve the creation of either a simulator or iteration of an existing browser extension which re-imagines Google’s Local OneBoxes? using more relevant content culled directly from organic. This new browser extension/simulator will be used to demonstrate to consumers, regulators and the media how Google’s current preferencing of its own properties hurts consumers and the 3rd party content ecosystem.
    • The Good News: Much of the ​heavy lifting has already been done -- engineers from Twitter and Facebook teamed up over a weekend to build an open-source plugin that reimagined a Google which didn't preference Google Plus social results. Their project had a profound effect on ​shaping consumer, regulator, and media sentiment away from Google. This would be building upon the world of ​focusontheuser.org and incorporating the Local category. I've ​even ​built ​mocks (<- 3 separate links there) of what a pro-competitive world would ​look like (Jeremy & Laurence approved). The fruits of this hackathon project, if successful, will be a critical piece of ammunition in protecting Yelp against Google's encroachment.
    • Why We Should Care
      • Fact: More than 65% of Yelp's web traffic comes from Google
      • Fact: Since 2007, Google has become increasingly aggressive in physically displacing Yelp (and other members of 3rd party local content ecosystem) in organic results as it promotes its own inferior products.
      • Fact: Google's promoted content is not even run through its own algorithm, but clumsily injected into SERP when a local query is detected, resulting in a de facto demotion of sites like Yelp.
      • Fact: According to Google, one third of all searches include a local intent. Such queries, which represent plurality of all search (WTF!), trigger an anti-competitive OneBox?.
      • Fact: In 2013, Google spent $20 million dollars in Federal lobbying alone, and it's working. Consider, the FTC's most recent "settlement" gave Google a license to be even more aggressive in displacing nascent competitors like Yelp. Legally speaking, the only thing stopping Mountain View from becoming more aggressive is the ongoing scrutiny from Texas's Attorney General and the European Commission, who are slated to release their own weak settlements within months.
